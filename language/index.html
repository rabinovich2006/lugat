Language

Qırımtatar tili

A Kipchak-Turkic language with three dialects, a thousand years of literature, and an illustrated dictionary for the next generation.

Crimean Tatar (Qırımtatar tili) is a Turkic language of the Kipchak-Cuman branch. It is the indigenous language of Crimea and is spoken today by roughly 540,000 people worldwide — in Crimea itself, in the post-deportation diaspora of Uzbekistan and Central Asia, in Turkey, Romania, Bulgaria, and elsewhere. UNESCO lists Crimean Tatar as severely endangered.

Linguistically, Crimean Tatar sits between the Kipchak languages of the steppe (Kazakh, Karachay-Balkar, Tatar) and the Oghuz languages of the Mediterranean basin (Turkish, Azerbaijani). This dual character reflects the geography of Crimea: a peninsula that has always faced both north into the steppe and south across the Black Sea.

The three dialects

Northern

Çöl şivesi · Steppe

Spoken across the plains of central and northern Crimea. The most conservative Kipchak dialect, closest to Nogay and Kazakh. Distinctive feature: word-initial /y-/ where the literary norm has /c-/ (e.g. yol vs literary col).

Middle

Orta şivesi · Foothills

Spoken in the central foothills around Bahçesaray, Aqmescit, and Karasubazar. The basis of the modern literary norm since the 1930s. Roughly balanced between Kipchak and Oghuz features.

Southern

Yalı boyu şivesi · Coast

Spoken along the southern coast from Aluşta to Sevastopol. Strongly influenced by Ottoman Turkish, with which it shares much vocabulary. Sometimes considered a separate language. Many speakers were killed or emigrated in the 19th century, then fully deported in 1944.

Alphabet

Crimean Tatar uses a 31-letter Latin alphabet (the official norm since 1997) as well as a Cyrillic alphabet still common in older publications and in the Russian-occupied territory. The Latin alphabet is the one used in this dictionary.

A few grammar notes

Agglutinative. Like all Turkic languages, Crimean Tatar builds words by stacking suffixes onto a stem. ev = house. ev-ler = houses. ev-ler-im = my houses. ev-ler-im-de = in my houses.

Vowel harmony. Suffixes change their vowel to match the last vowel of the stem. at-lar (horses), but köz-ler (eyes) — the suffix -lar/-ler chooses to match.

SOV word order. Subject–object–verb is the default: Men kitap oqudum — "I a book read" (= I read a book).

No grammatical gender. Unlike Russian, Crimean Tatar nouns and pronouns are not gendered. The third-person pronoun o means he, she, or it.

Possessive on the thing, not the owner. "My house" is evim — literally "house-my". This is one of the most common Turkic patterns.
