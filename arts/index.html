Arts & Crafts

Ornek ve Sanat

Ornek — recognised by UNESCO in 2021 — and the wider universe of Crimean Tatar visual tradition.

Ornek — the Crimean Tatar ornamental art — was inscribed by UNESCO on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2021. It lives in embroidery, jewellery, ceramics, copperwork, and architecture, with motifs that are read like an alphabet.

The Fountain of Tears · Selsebil

The marble fountain in the Khan's Palace, immortalised in Pushkin's poem. Its drops are read as a metaphor for grief — a small daily Ornek of stone, water, and time.

Beyond Ornek

Crimean Tatar craft tradition also encompasses filigree silver and gold jewellery (especially the chest pendants worn by women), woven kilims, blue-and-white ceramic plates of the Bahçesaray school, copper trays engraved by master artisans, and the carved wooden panels of the Khan's Palace.

Eight essential motifs

Almali

Apple

Symbol of fertility and abundance — found in embroidery, ceramics, and architectural decoration. Often surrounded by leaves and tendrils.

Çiçek

Flower (rose, tulip, carnation)

The most widespread Ornek motif. Each flower carries its own symbolism: the carnation for love, the tulip for the divine, the rose for paradise.

Yıldız

Star

Eight- and twelve-pointed stars are central to Crimean Tatar ornament. They mark sacred space and continuity with Islamic geometric tradition.

Yelpez

Fan

A spreading fan shape, often used in border ornaments and in jewellery filigree.

Tuhum

Egg

Symbol of life and new beginnings — common in spring textiles and Hıdırlez decoration.

Selvi

Cypress tree

Symbol of eternal life and remembrance — often paired with a bird or moon. Found in funerary embroidery.

Boynuz

Horns

Stylised ram's horns, sign of strength and protection. Frequent on men's belts, saddles, and copperware.

Su

Water (running waves)

A wave motif representing rivers, fountains, and the Black Sea. Lines the borders of textiles.
