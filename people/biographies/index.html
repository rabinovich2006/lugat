Noman Çelebicihan

1885–1918
Poet · First Mufti of Crimea · President of the Crimean People's Republic

In English

Noman Çelebicihan was a Crimean Tatar poet, lawyer, and statesman best known for two enduring legacies: authoring the Crimean Tatar national anthem "Ant etkenmen" ("I have sworn"), and serving as the first elected Mufti of Crimea and President of the short-lived Crimean People's Republic.

Born in Sonaq village in the Or (Perekop) district, he studied at the Zincirli Medrese in Bahçesaray, then took degrees in theology in Istanbul and law at the Petersburg University. He emerged during the upheaval of 1917 as the leading voice of Crimean Tatar national self-determination.

In December 1917, the Qurultay — the first democratic Crimean Tatar parliament — elected Çelebicihan head of state of the Crimean People's Republic, one of the earliest secular democratic republics in the Muslim world. Its constitution granted women full voting rights. Within weeks Bolshevik forces overran Crimea; Çelebicihan was arrested in early 1918, executed in Sevastopol on 23 February, and his body cast into the Black Sea.

His anthem, written in 1917, remains the official anthem of the Crimean Tatar people today. Its opening line — "Ant etkenmen, milletimniñ yarasını sarmağa" ("I have sworn to bind the wounds of my people") — is one of the most recognised lines in Crimean Tatar literature.

İsmail Gaspıralı

1851–1914
Educator · Publisher · Pan-Turkic reformer

In English

İsmail Gaspıralı (also Gasprinski) is considered the father of modern Crimean Tatar identity and one of the most influential Muslim intellectuals of the late Russian Empire. His motto — "Dilde, fikirde, işte birlik" ("Unity in language, thought, and deed") — became the rallying cry of the early-twentieth-century pan-Turkic reform movement.

Born near Bahçesaray in 1851, he was educated in Moscow, Istanbul, and Paris before returning to Crimea, where he served briefly as mayor of Bahçesaray (1879–1884). In 1883 he founded "Tercüman" ("The Interpreter"), the first Turkic-language newspaper in the Russian Empire. Published bilingually in Crimean Tatar and Russian, it ran continuously until 1918 and reached readers from the Volga to the Aral Sea, shaping a shared Turkic intellectual public sphere.

Gaspıralı pioneered the Jadid ("new-method") school movement: a phonetic, child-centred curriculum that replaced rote Quranic memorisation. By the early 1900s, more than five thousand Jadid schools were operating across the Russian Empire and beyond. He also championed women's education and edited the women's journal "Alem-i Nisvan" ("Women's World"), one of the earliest of its kind in the Muslim world.

He died in 1914 and was buried in the Zincirli Medrese cemetery in Bahçesaray. UNESCO marked the 100th anniversary of his death in 2014.

Bekir Çoban-zade

1893–1937
Philologist · Turkologist · Poet

In English

Bekir Çoban-zade was the leading Turkologist of his generation and the architect of modern Crimean Tatar as a literary language. Born to a shepherd's family in Arğın village near Kefe (Feodosia), he was educated at the Galatasaray Lyceum in Istanbul and then at the University of Budapest, where he earned a doctorate in Turkic philology in 1920.

Returning to Crimea, he became a professor at Tavrida University, founded the first Crimean Tatar academic literary journal, and led the standardisation of the Crimean Tatar literary norm — including the orthography reforms of the 1920s. He also produced influential studies of mediaeval Turkic literature, including the first scholarly editions of works by Mahmud al-Kashgari and Yusuf Balasaguni.

His own poetry — collected in volumes such as "Boran" ("Storm") — is sparse, modernist, and politically charged. He fell victim to Stalinist purges: arrested in 1937 on fabricated charges of pan-Turkism and "bourgeois nationalism", he was executed in Baku on 13 October 1937. He was officially rehabilitated in 1957.

Abdulla Lâtif-zade

1890–1938
Poet · Educator

In English

Abdulla Lâtif-zade was a key figure in the first generation of Crimean Tatar Soviet-era poets. Orphaned young, he was raised by an uncle, completed the local rüştiye and lyceum in Aqmescit (Simferopol), and went on to study in Istanbul and Petersburg.

His verse blends late-Ottoman lyrical forms with early-Soviet free-verse experimentation, treating themes of land, displacement, and folk memory. He taught and edited in 1920s Crimea alongside Çoban-zade and the Jadid circle.

He was arrested in the same wave of purges as Çoban-zade in 1937 and executed the following year. His work was banned for decades and only re-published in Crimea in the late Soviet period.

Cemil Kermençikli

1891–1942
Poet · Public figure

In English

Cemil Kermençikli, born in Kermençik village in the Yalta district, was a self-taught poet from a teacher's family. He was active in early-twentieth-century Crimean Tatar cultural life, contributed to the Bahçesaray press founded by Gaspıralı, and helped organise Crimean Tatar civic associations in the 1910s and 1920s.

His lyrical verse — much of it patriotic and elegiac — circulated widely in manuscript and periodicals before formal publication. He perished in the Soviet system in 1942, and many of his works survived only in copies preserved abroad and later returned home.

C

Cengiz Dağcı

1920–2011
Novelist of the Deportation generation

In English

Cengiz Dağcı is the most widely read Crimean Tatar novelist of the twentieth century. Born in Gurzuf in 1920, he was conscripted into the Red Army in 1941, captured by German forces, survived the war as a prisoner, and eventually settled in London in 1946 — never to set foot in Crimea again.

Writing in Turkish, he produced a series of novels that brought the Crimean Tatar tragedy to a global audience: "Korkunç Yıllar" ("Terrible Years", 1956) and "Yurdunu Kaybeden Adam" ("The Man Who Lost His Homeland", 1957) are among the most-translated works in Turkish literature. "Onlar da İnsandı" ("They Were People Too", 1958) is a searing chronicle of the 18 May 1944 sürgün — the deportation of the entire Crimean Tatar nation.

He died in London in 2011 and, in accordance with his wishes, was reburied in Gurzuf — finally returning to the homeland whose loss he had spent six decades describing.

Original biography · Qırımtatarca

Onı bir qaç ay apishanede “duşman sıfatında” tutalar ve serbest bıraqalar. Emirüsein Qızıltaşqa bir daa qaytmay, Aqmescitte qala, berberhanelerden birinde berberlik yapa. Cengiz Dağcı başlanğıç mektepni 1931 senesi Qızıltaşta bitire. 1932 senesiniñ baarinde babası Cengizni yanına çağırtıp ala.

Sabriye Erecepova

1912–1977
Singer of Crimean Tatar folk and classical music

In English

Sabriye Erecepova is often called the legendary singer ("efsaneviy yırcı") of the Crimean Tatars. Born in Bahçesaray to a musical family, she joined the State Crimean Tatar Drama Theatre and the Bahçesaray Ensemble in the 1930s, recording her first records before the war.

Deported to Uzbekistan with her people in 1944, she made Tashkent the centre of Crimean Tatar music in exile, performing folk songs ("chın" and "qaytarma"), classical maqam pieces, and the soundtrack of a culture that the Soviet state had erased from the map. Her interpretations of "Ey güzel Qırım" and "Şu Bağçada" became anthems of the diaspora.

She died in Tashkent in 1977 and was buried in the Dombrabad cemetery, never having received permission to return to Crimea.

In English

Abdureşid Mediyev was a leader of the Crimean Tatar reform movement of the 1900s. Born to a poor peasant family in the Or district, he trained as a lawyer and became one of the most articulate political voices of his generation.

Burada kısa süre içinde ağır hastalıklar ve açlık nedeniyle anne, baba ve yakın akrabalarının hepsi öldü. İdris, kız kardeşi ile yaşamaya devam etti. İdris Asanin’in yaşadığı büyük trajedi, kitlesel ölümler, yabancı ülkede yaşamak onun son günlerine kadar onu derinden yaraladı.

Ş

Şamil Alâdin

1912–1996
Writer · Editor of "Yıldız" journal

In English

Şamil Alâdin was a central figure of twentieth-century Crimean Tatar prose. Born in Mahuldür village near Yalta in 1912, he studied at the Bahçesaray seven-year school and, like many of his generation, came to Crimean Tatar literature through Bekir Çoban-zade.

After serving in the Second World War he found his deported family in Uzbekistan and stayed with them, working as a writer and editor. From 1980 to 1985 he served as editor-in-chief of "Yıldız" ("The Star"), the principal Crimean Tatar literary journal of the late Soviet period — a role that made him a quiet custodian of the language through decades of exile.

His two-volume Selected Works appeared in 1976–1977. He died in 1996, three years after returning to Crimea.

Original biography · Qırımtatarca

Şamil Alâdin 1912 senesiniñ iyül 12-de Yalta uyezdiniñ Mahuldür köyünde doğdı. Bağçasarayda yedi yıllıq mektepni bitirdi. Anda Şamil o zamannıñ belli ocası Yağya Bayburtlıdan ders ala, edebiyatqa sevgi ve aveslikni de oña şu ocası aşlay.

Bu araştıruvlar neticesinde müellif şunı añladı ki, oña öz fikirlerini istegeni kibi tolu ve keniş sürette ifade etmek içün nesirge baş urmaq kerek. Em öyle de yapa. Soñundan basılğan bir sıra nesir eserleri onıñ bu fikriniñ doğrulığını tasdiqladılar.

Mında o biraz vaqıt yaş temaşıcılar teatriniñ direktorı vazifesinde çalıştı, soñra 1949 senesinden başlap çoq yıllar devamında Özbekistan yazıcıları birliginiñ apparatında edebiy konsultant, mesül kâtip olıp çalıştı. 1965–1966 seneleri az vaqıt “Lenin bayrağı” muarririniñ muavini oldı, soñ kene Yazıcılar birligine işke keçti. 1980–1985 seneleri ise “Yıldız” mecmuasınıñ baş muarriri oldı.

Bunda onıñ romanları ve ayrı povestleri kirsetildi. 1987 senesi ise edipniñ “Çoqraçıqlar” adlı büyük kölemde saylama eserleri kitabı neşir etildi. Edipniñ özüne ise Özbekistanda nam qazanğan medeniyet hadimi ve “Özbekistanda nam qazanğan sanat erbabı” kibi şerifli unvanlar berildi. Böylelikle, ilki nesir eserlerini cenkten evel bastırğan Şamil Alâdin umumen cenkten soñki qırımtatar edebiyatında iri nesirci sıfatında icat etip keldi. Şamil Alâdin 1994 senesi Vatanğa qaytıp keldi ve mında gazetalarda aktual mevzularda bir sıra publitsistik maqalelernen çıqışta bulundı.

Edip 1996 senesi mayıs 21-de, 84 yaşını toldurmadan vefat etti, Aqmescitniñ Abdal mezarlığında defin etildi.

Osman Aqçoqraqlı

1878–1938
Historian · Ethnographer · Archaeologist

In English

Osman Aqçoqraqlı was the leading Crimean Tatar historian and ethnographer of the early Soviet period. Born in Bahçesaray in 1878 — his family name derives from Aqçoqraq village — he wrote literary criticism, edited periodicals, and pioneered scholarly archaeology of the Crimean Khanate.

He took his first steps in literature while serving in the imperial army; his historical sketch "Nenekecan Hanım Türbesi" appeared in St Petersburg in 1899. Over the next three decades he produced studies of Crimean Khan-era epigraphy, mosque architecture, and folk literature, and helped found the State Bahçesaray Palace Museum (today the Khan's Palace).

Arrested during the Stalinist purges of 1937, he was executed in 1938. He was rehabilitated posthumously in 1989.

M

Memet Niyazi

1878–1931
Crimean Tatar poet of the Dobruja diaspora

In English

Memet Niyazi was the central literary voice of the Crimean Tatar community in Dobruja — the population that fled to Romanian and Ottoman lands after the 1860s Russian annexation of Crimea. Born in 1878 in the village of Aşçılar in Romania, he received his early education there.

He returned to Crimea in 1898, then continued his training at Istanbul's Darü'l-Muallimîn (teachers' college) in 1899. Back in Dobruja, he taught Crimean Tatar children, edited the periodicals "Dobruca Sadası" and "Teşvik", and wrote patriotic verse that gave the Crimean Tatar diaspora a sense of itself.

He died in 1931. His poems are still recited at Crimean Tatar gatherings in Romania, Turkey, and Crimea today.

A

Amdi Giraybay

1901–1930
Poet · Scholar of Crimean Tatar literature

In English

Amdi Giraybay was a poet and folklorist of the brief 1920s Crimean Tatar cultural renaissance. Born in Yeñi Sala village in the Kefe district to a family of religious scholars, he completed the Qarasuvbazar elementary school in 1910 and went on to higher studies in Crimea and Turkey.

He published poetry collections in the 1920s and worked as a researcher of Crimean Tatar folk literature — collecting destans, songs, and proverbs that would otherwise have been lost. His work was charged with the same modernist energy as that of Çoban-zade and Lâtif-zade.

He died young, in 1930, before the worst of the Stalinist purges — a small mercy in a generation that was almost entirely erased.
