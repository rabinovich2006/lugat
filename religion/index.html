Religion & Spiritual Life

Din ve Maneviyat

Sunni Hanafi tradition, Sufi heritage, the great mosques, and the folk practices that sit alongside them.

Sunni Hanafi Islam

The Crimean Tatar people have been predominantly Sunni Muslim, following the Hanafi school, since at least the 14th century. Islam came to Crimea through the Golden Horde and was consolidated under the Khanate.

The mosques of Crimea

The Han Cami (Khan's Mosque) in Bahçesaray, completed in 1532 under Sahib I Geray, remains the spiritual heart of the Crimean Tatar people. The Cuma-Cami (Friday Mosque) in Yevpatoriya, built by the great Ottoman architect Mimar Sinan in 1564, is one of the finest mosques in the Black Sea region. Both survived the Soviet period — repurposed, neglected, but not destroyed — and remain in active use today.

Sufi heritage

Crimea was historically influenced by the Naqshbandi, Mawlawi, and Bektashi Sufi orders. Sufi tekkes (lodges) existed in many Crimean Tatar towns into the 20th century. The folk-religious tradition of zikr songs, visiting of saintly tombs, and the festival of Hıdırlez all retain Sufi roots.

Folk religion and syncretism

Alongside the formal practice of Islam, Crimean Tatar religious life has long preserved older Turkic and local elements: the veneration of certain springs and trees, the springtime festival of Hıdırlez (associated with the figures of Hızır and İlyas), seasonal ceremonies of rain-making and harvest. These layers coexist comfortably with the mainstream Islamic calendar.

Religious holidays

The major holidays are Ramazan Bayramı (end of Ramadan), Qurban Bayramı (Eid al-Adha), Mevlid (the Prophet's birthday), and the syncretic Hıdırlez. The Friday prayer at Han Cami in Bahçesaray remains a central act of communal life.
