Library & Resources

Kütüphane

A curated bibliography, documentary films, podcasts, and active cultural organisations.

A curated reading list, films, podcasts, and active cultural organisations. If you know of a resource that belongs here, please open an issue at the project repository or email velibakalov@gmail.com.

Books — history

  • Brian Glyn Williams, The Crimean Tatars: From Soviet Genocide to Putin's Conquest (Oxford University Press, 2015). The most accessible single-volume English-language history of the Crimean Tatars from the Khanate to the 2014 annexation, by the leading Western scholar of the subject.
  • Greta Lynn Uehling, Beyond Memory: The Crimean Tatars' Deportation and Return (Palgrave, 2004). Anthropological study of the deportation generation and the experience of return, based on extensive fieldwork in Crimea in the 1990s.
  • Edward Allworth (ed.), The Tatars of Crimea: Return to the Homeland (Duke University Press, 1998). The standard academic collection of essays on Crimean Tatar history, language, and politics — co-written with the participation of Mustafa Cemilev.
  • Alan Fisher, The Crimean Tatars (Hoover Institution Press, 1978). The first English-language scholarly monograph; still essential for the pre-Soviet period.
  • Sevgi Karakaya, Çelebicihan ve Onun Devri (Bahçesaray, 2017). Detailed biographical study of Noman Çelebicihan and the 1917 Qurultay.

Books — literature

  • Cengiz Dağcı, Korkunç Yıllar ("Terrible Years," 1956) — a young man's first-person account of war, capture, and the discovery that his family has been deported. The opening novel of Crimean Tatar literature in Turkish.
  • Cengiz Dağcı, Yurdunu Kaybeden Adam ("The Man Who Lost His Homeland," 1957) — sequel; the protagonist's life as a displaced person in postwar Europe.
  • Cengiz Dağcı, Onlar da İnsandı ("They Were People Too," 1958) — the most direct novel of the 1944 deportation; a foundational text of Crimean Tatar memory.
  • Veli Bakalov, Balaçıqlar İçün Resimli Luğat (2019). The trilingual illustrated children's dictionary that is the basis of the Language section of this site.
  • Şamil Alâdin, Eserler (Selected Works, 1976–1977, 2 vols). Soviet-era novels and essays by the long-time editor of Yıldız.
  • Eşref Şemi-zade, Qoqlay (1962) — epic verse by the major Soviet-era Crimean Tatar poet.
  • Bekir Çoban-zade, Boran (1929) — the modernist poetry collection of the man who built the Crimean Tatar literary norm; banned and destroyed after his execution in 1937, recovered and republished in the 1990s.

Films

  • Haytarma (2013), dir. Akhtem Seitablaev. The first dramatic feature film about the 1944 deportation. The protagonist is the Crimean Tatar twice-Hero-of-the-Soviet-Union pilot Amet-Han Sultan, who in the film returns to Crimea on leave in May 1944 and witnesses the deportation of his own family.
  • Mustafa (2018) — documentary on the life of Mustafa Cemilev, fifteen years of Soviet imprisonment, and the half-century struggle for the right of return.
  • Çoçaklarımız ("Our Children," 2021) — short documentary on Crimean Tatar language teachers in occupied Crimea, working under increasing restriction.
  • 1944 (2015) — Estonian feature film on the wider Soviet deportations of nationalities, with significant Crimean Tatar narrative material.

Active organisations & media

  • QHA — Crimean News Agency. Reporting in Crimean Tatar, Ukrainian, English, and Russian; based in Kyiv since the 2014 annexation.
  • ATR TV — the first Crimean Tatar television channel, founded 2006 by Lenür Islâmov. Forced into exile from Crimea in 2015, now broadcasting from Kyiv.
  • Mejlis of the Crimean Tatar People — the executive body of the Crimean Tatar Qurultay. Banned by Russian authorities in 2016; continues to operate from Ukrainian territory.
  • Crimean Tatar Resource Center — Kyiv-based human-rights and research NGO documenting violations in occupied Crimea.
  • Suspilne Crimea — Ukrainian public broadcaster's Crimean Tatar service.
  • Hromadske TV — Ukrainian independent broadcaster with extensive Crimean Tatar reporting.
  • QIRIM — Turkish state radio's Crimean Tatar programming.

Academic & archival resources

  • Krymology — multilingual encyclopaedia of Crimean studies (Crimean Tatar / Ukrainian / English / Russian).
  • Crimean Tatar Oral History Archive — Columbia University Libraries, Slavic and East European Studies. Hundreds of audio interviews with deportation survivors and their descendants.
  • University of Toronto, Munk School — extensive collection of Crimean Tatar samizdat (underground publications) from the 1960s–80s national movement.
  • UNESCO: Ornek and the knowledge associated with it (2021) — official inscription on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. The full nomination file is downloadable from ich.unesco.org.
  • UNESCO: Hıdırlez (spring festival) (2022) — joint inscription with the Republic of Turkey.

Contribute

This is an open cultural project under continuous expansion. We welcome:

  • Family recipes — especially for kobete, samsa, kavurma, and the regional variations of çibörek
  • Survivor testimonies for the Sürgün archive (in any language; we will translate)
  • Public-domain photographs of Crimean Tatar life, places, and people
  • Crimean Tatar lyrics, lullabies, and folk tales not yet covered
  • Corrections of any kind, particularly to the language and dialect sections

Open an issue at github.com/rabinovich2006/lugat or email velibakalov@gmail.com.
