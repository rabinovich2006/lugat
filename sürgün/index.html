Sürgün · 18 Mayıs 1944

Voices of Sürgün

Sürgün sesleri

A testimonial archive for the 1944 deportation and the long exile that followed.

What happened on 18 May 1944

Beginning at 3 a.m. on 18 May, NKVD troops in pre-positioned units across all of Crimea simultaneously entered Crimean Tatar villages. Families were given between fifteen minutes and a few hours to gather what they could carry. They were marched to railway sidings where freight trains — cattle wagons normally used for transporting livestock — were waiting. By the morning of 20 May, almost the entire Crimean Tatar population of Crimea had been placed on those trains. The order — State Defence Committee decree No. 5859ss — had been signed by Stalin nine days earlier.

The journey

The trains travelled east for between two and four weeks, depending on destination. Each wagon held between 100 and 150 people, with no toilets, almost no water, and only the bread the deportees had managed to bring. People died from heat, dehydration, dysentery, and shock. The bodies of the dead were thrown out at brief station stops, often without prayer or burial. Crimean Tatar memory records this period as Yol — "the Road."

The first years in exile

The survivors were placed under the spetsposelenie regime — special-settlement camp law — in rural districts of Uzbekistan (the majority), the Ural region, Kazakhstan, and Siberia. They were forbidden to leave their assigned villages without written permission; they were forbidden to speak Crimean Tatar in public; the children were taught only in Russian. Crimean Tatar identity ceased, by Soviet decree, to legally exist. Between May 1944 and the spring of 1947, between 100,000 and 110,000 Crimean Tatars — close to half of the deported population — died.

Testimonies

Below are short excerpts from testimonies gathered by Crimean Tatar oral historians, by the Crimean Tatar Resource Center, and by independent journalists since 1989. A more complete archive with audio and full transcripts is in development — if you or your family have a story to share, please reach out through the Library page.

"They came at three in the morning. They gave us fifteen minutes. They said we were going for a week. My mother packed bread, dried fruit, and the Quran. She left the door open. We never came back."
Zarema Abibulaeva, born 1932 in Bakhçesaray district · Recorded 1996, Tashkent
"On the train I learned what death smells like. By the third day there were five of us children, but only three of us were alive. The soldiers opened the doors only once a day, sometimes less. My mother sang to me to keep me from crying."
Server Reşitov, born 1939 in Aluşta · Recorded 2003, Aqmescit
"My father was at the front. He had killed three Germans with his own hands at the Battle of Kerch. While he was fighting, his mother, his sister, and his two children were put on the train. He came home in 1945 to find his village renamed and his family scattered across Central Asia. It took him four years to find his children."
Anonymous, son of a Red Army officer · Submitted 2018 to the Crimean Tatar Resource Center
"At Andijan they put us off the train. There was nothing — no shelter, no food. The Uzbek farmers had been told we were traitors, but when they saw us — when they saw the children — they shared their bread. They taught us to plant cotton. We learned their language. We never forgot the bread."
Lutfiye Aliyeva, born 1937 in Kefe · Recorded 2009, Andijan
"My grandmother lived to ninety-three. She returned to Crimea in 1990, at eighty. The house that had been our family's was occupied by another family. She did not ask them to leave. She walked instead to the cemetery and looked for her parents' graves. They had been ploughed under. She sat on the ground and said, 'I am here. I am home. I am here.'"
From the family memoir of an unnamed contributor · Aqmescit, 2014

The toll

The exact number of Crimean Tatar deaths from the deportation has been debated for eighty years. The most rigorous demographic studies — including those published by Russian historians since 1991 — converge on a figure of between 18% and 46% of the deported population dying in the first three years, with most estimates clustering around 110,000 deaths from a pre-deportation population of roughly 240,000.

In 2015 the Verkhovna Rada of Ukraine formally recognised the 1944 deportation as an act of genocide against the Crimean Tatar people. The European Parliament passed a similar recognition in 2016. Russian authorities — the legal successor to the Soviet state — have never apologised.
