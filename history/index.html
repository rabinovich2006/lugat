History

TarihИстория

From the foundation of the Crimean Khanate in 1441 to the present day — five centuries of statehood, two centuries of dispossession, half a century of deportation, and a return that is still under way.

c. 4th c. BCE

Steppe and shore

The Crimean peninsula sits between the Eurasian steppe and the Black Sea — a meeting point of Scythians, Greeks, Goths, Sarmatians, Khazars, Cumans, and many others. The Crimean Tatar people emerge from this long mingling, with the Turkic Cuman-Kipchak language and culture forming their core layer.

1239

The Mongol arrival

Crimea is brought into the Mongol Empire under Batu Khan, and then into the Jochid (Golden Horde) ulus. The peninsula's Turkic-Mongol elite, Kipchak-speaking population, and the older settled communities together shape the basis of the Crimean Tatar nation.

1441

Foundation of the Crimean Khanate

Hacı Geray declares the Crimean Khanate independent of the Golden Horde, establishing a Crimean Tatar state that will last more than three centuries. The Giray dynasty traces its descent from Genghis Khan; the capital is Bahçesaray (the "garden palace").

"Sahib Geray Han Tahta Mindi" — "Sahib Geray Khan sat upon the throne."
Crimean Tatar historical chronicle, 16th c.
1475

Ottoman alliance

Following Ottoman intervention, the Khanate becomes a vassal of the Ottoman Empire while keeping internal autonomy and an elected Khan. For the next three centuries the Khanate is one of the most important political and cultural centres of Eastern Europe.

1532

Sahib Geray and Bahçesaray

Khan Sahib Geray builds the Khan's Palace (Hansaray) in Bahçesaray, the centre of Crimean Tatar political and cultural life. Around it grow mosques, medreses, fountains, and the famous "Fountain of Tears" — later immortalised by Pushkin.

1783

Russian annexation

Catherine II of Russia annexes the Crimean Khanate, ending more than three centuries of Crimean Tatar statehood. Land is confiscated and redistributed to Russian nobility; mosques and waqfs are seized.

What follows is the first sürgünlük — the first emigration. Over the next century, several hundred thousand Crimean Tatars leave for the Ottoman Empire, settling in Anatolia, Dobruja, and the Balkans. The Crimean Tatar share of Crimea's population, which had been overwhelming, falls steadily.

1854–1856

Crimean War and the second emigration

Russian authorities suspect Crimean Tatars of sympathy with the Ottoman-British-French alliance. After the war, persecution drives a new wave of emigration to the Ottoman lands — perhaps 200,000 people, including most of the Crimean Tatar nobility and clergy.

1883

Gaspıralı founds Tercüman

In Bahçesaray, İsmail Gaspıralı launches "Tercüman" ("The Interpreter") — the first Turkic-language newspaper in the Russian Empire. Published in Crimean Tatar and Russian for thirty-five years, it sparks a Jadid reform movement that reshapes Muslim education across Russia and Central Asia.

Gaspıralı's slogan — "Dilde, fikirde, işte birlik" ("Unity in language, thought, and deed") — becomes the rallying cry of an entire generation.

"A nation lives in its language."
İsmail Gaspıralı
Dec 1917

First Qurultay & Crimean People's Republic

Riding the wave of the February Revolution, Crimean Tatars elect their first democratic parliament — the Qurultay — in Bahçesaray. It declares the Crimean People's Republic, with Noman Çelebicihan as president. Its constitution guarantees women's suffrage, one of the earliest in the Muslim world.

The republic lasts only weeks before Bolshevik forces overrun Crimea.

"I have sworn to bind the wounds of my people. / If they do not heal, let me die in misery."
Noman Çelebicihan, "Ant etkenmen" (1917) — Crimean Tatar national anthem
23 Feb 1918

Execution of Çelebicihan

Noman Çelebicihan — Mufti of Crimea, president of the People's Republic, author of the national anthem — is shot by Bolsheviks in Sevastopol and his body cast into the Black Sea.

1921

Crimean ASSR

After the Russian Civil War, the Soviets establish the Crimean Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic. Crimean Tatar is recognised as a state language alongside Russian, and a brief cultural renaissance follows — newspapers, schools, theatre, the Yıldız literary journal.

1928–1938

Stalinist purges

The Soviet leadership turns on the very intelligentsia it had nurtured. Veli İbraimov, the Crimean Tatar leader of the 1920s republic, is executed in 1928 on fabricated charges. Throughout the 1930s, hundreds of writers, teachers, religious scholars, and political figures are arrested, deported, or shot.

Bekir Çoban-zade, Abdulla Lâtif-zade, Osman Aqçoqraqlı, and many others perish in this period.

18 May 1944

Sürgünlik — The Deportation

By order of Stalin, the entire Crimean Tatar people — every man, woman, child, elder, and infant, including soldiers serving in the Red Army — is deported from Crimea on accusations of collaboration with Nazi forces. The operation, carried out by NKVD troops in roughly three days, transports more than 190,000 people in sealed cattle cars to Uzbekistan, the Urals, and Siberia.

An estimated 18,000 to 46,000 die during transport. By 1949 nearly half of the deported population — approximately 100,000 people — has died of starvation, disease, and exhaustion in the special settlements (spetsposelenie) where they are held under camp regime.

Crimean Tatar villages are renamed in Russian. Their cemeteries are destroyed. Their language vanishes from schools. The Soviet state officially declares the Crimean Tatars to have ceased to exist as a nation.

"They came at three in the morning. They gave us fifteen minutes. They sealed us in cars meant for animals."
Survivor testimony
1956

Khrushchev's secret speech — Crimean Tatars excluded

Khrushchev acknowledges the deportations of the Chechens, Ingush, Kalmyks, Karachays, and Balkars, and these peoples are gradually permitted to return home. The Crimean Tatars are pointedly excluded from this rehabilitation. The campaign for the right of return begins immediately, organised quietly from the spetsposelenie.

1960s–1980s

The national movement

A grassroots Crimean Tatar national movement — one of the largest civic movements in the Soviet Union — organises mass petitions, samizdat, and unauthorised gatherings demanding the right of return. Leaders such as Mustafa Cemilev, who would later become one of the great human-rights figures of the late USSR, are repeatedly imprisoned.

Crimean Tatar identity is sustained in exile through family memory, music, and the small literary journal Yıldız in Tashkent.

"Either we return, or we die where we are. We will not be erased."
Mustafa Cemilev
1987

Red Square demonstration

On 23 July 1987, several hundred Crimean Tatars stage a public protest on Red Square in Moscow demanding the right to return. It is one of the most public demonstrations of the late Soviet era. International coverage forces the Soviet leadership to convene a state commission on the question.

1989–1991

The return begins

With the easing of Soviet restrictions, the Crimean Tatar people begin returning home en masse. Several hundred thousand make the journey from Uzbekistan and Central Asia back to Crimea — often to find their villages renamed and their old houses inhabited by others. Land disputes, squatter settlements ("samozakhvat"), and frequent discrimination follow.

Ukraine, newly independent, gradually establishes legal mechanisms for the return — though the process remains contested.

1991

Second Qurultay and the Mejlis

The Crimean Tatar Qurultay convenes for the second time in history, after a 74-year interruption. It re-establishes the Mejlis — the executive body of the Crimean Tatar people — and adopts a declaration of national sovereignty within an independent Ukraine. Mustafa Cemilev is elected chair.

March 2014

Russian annexation of Crimea

Following the Maidan revolution in Kyiv, Russian forces seize Crimea. A staged referendum, held under military occupation and not recognised by the international community, is used to justify annexation.

The Mejlis is the only Crimean Tatar representative body to formally oppose the annexation. Its leaders Mustafa Cemilev and Refat Çubarov are banned from entering Crimea by the Russian authorities.

26 April 2016

Mejlis banned

Russia's Supreme Court declares the Mejlis of the Crimean Tatar People an "extremist organisation" and bans it on the territory it occupies. Activists, journalists, and lawyers face dozens of arrests, raids, and politically motivated prosecutions in subsequent years.

May 2016

Jamala wins Eurovision with "1944"

Crimean Tatar singer Jamala wins the Eurovision Song Contest with her song "1944" — a song about her great-grandmother's deportation. The performance brings the Crimean Tatar tragedy to a global audience of two hundred million viewers.

"When strangers are coming, they come to your house. / They kill you all and say: 'we are not guilty.'"
Jamala, "1944"
2021

Ornek inscribed by UNESCO

UNESCO inscribes "Ornek — the Crimean Tatar ornamental art and the knowledge associated with it" on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. The recognition gives international protection to the embroidery, jewellery, ceramic, and architectural traditions of the Crimean Tatar people.

Today

A people still returning

Roughly 300,000 Crimean Tatars live in Crimea, with a further half a million in Uzbekistan, Turkey, and a global diaspora. The struggle for political, cultural, and religious rights continues — in occupied Crimea, in Ukrainian and international courts, and in the everyday work of language teachers, recipe keepers, musicians, and parents.

This site is one small act of that continuing work.
