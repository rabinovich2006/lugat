Festivals & Traditions

Bayramlar

Hıdırlez and Navrez, weddings and namings, and the May 18 commemoration of Sürgün.

The Crimean Tatar year carries Islamic feasts, Turkic seasonal celebrations, and one solemn day of remembrance — woven together into a calendar of work, rest, prayer, and memory.

Late spring — 5–6 May

Hıdırlez

The major spring festival, celebrating the awakening of nature and the meeting of the saints Hızır and İlyas. People dress in their best clothes, gather in the meadows, light bonfires, and roll Easter-style decorated eggs down hills. Children jump fires and make wishes.

Spring equinox — 21 March

Navrez

The Crimean Tatar New Year, shared with many Iranic and Turkic peoples. Houses are cleaned, new clothes are made, the table holds seven "S" foods (samanu, sumak, sirke, sib, sir, sumbül, sikke) — a feast that welcomes the year to come.

Autumn — September/October

Derviza

The harvest festival. Communities mark the end of the agricultural year with thanksgiving, sheep-shearing games, and traditional wrestling. The festival was suppressed under Soviet rule and revived after 1991.

18 May

Sürgün Day

Sürgünlik Künü — the day of mourning. Crimean Tatars worldwide gather to commemorate the 1944 deportation. Black ribbons, candle vigils, public readings of survivor testimonies, and the unanswerable question: what could have been?

Variable (Islamic calendar)

Ramazan Bayramı

The festival ending the holy month of Ramazan. Houses are cleaned, sweets are prepared, children visit elders and receive small gifts. Special prayers at the Han Cami in Bahçesaray and other historic mosques.

Variable (Islamic calendar)

Qurban Bayramı

The festival of sacrifice, when families share lamb with neighbours, the poor, and extended family. Ritual prayer at sunrise, three days of visits and feasting.

Hıdırlez — the great spring festival

Hıdırlez (or Ederlez) falls on the first weekend of May, when the Crimean steppe is in full bloom and the last frost is past. It honours the meeting of two saints from the folk-Islamic tradition — Hızır (the green one, who walks the earth) and İlyas (Elijah, who rides the seas) — believed to meet once a year on this day and bless the land.

Traditional Hıdırlez customs:

  • Wishes tied to a tree. Each family member ties a strip of cloth to a young tree, whispering a wish. The wish is said to come true if the cloth still hangs at next year's Hıdırlez.
  • Jumping over the bonfire. Young people jump a bonfire seven times — for health, marriage, fertility, and the dispelling of the previous year's grief.
  • Rolling decorated eggs. Eggs dyed yellow with onion skins are rolled down a small hill; the one whose egg rolls the furthest is said to receive the year's blessing.
  • Communal food. Lambs are slaughtered and the meat is shared with neighbours, the elderly, and the poor. Special breads — qalaqay — are baked in clay ovens.
  • Music and dance until late. Haytarma circles form on the meadows; the saz, the dare, and the davul sound from sundown to midnight.

In 2022, UNESCO inscribed the Hıdırlez festival on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity, jointly with the spring festival of the Turkish people.

Navrez — the Crimean Tatar new year

Navrez (Nawrūz) falls on the spring equinox, 21 March, marking the new year and the beginning of agricultural work. Like Hıdırlez it carries pre-Islamic Turkic and Iranian roots that have settled comfortably alongside the Islamic calendar.

Houses are cleaned from top to bottom. The Navrez table holds seven foods whose Crimean Tatar names begin with the letter S — samanu (sprouted wheat pudding), sumak, sirke (vinegar), sib (apple), sir (garlic), sümbül (hyacinth), and sikke (coin) — each a wish for the coming year. Visitors carry small gifts of dried fruit and nuts.

Crimean Tatar wedding customs

A Crimean Tatar wedding is a sequence of days, not a single event.

  1. Qız istev — "asking for the girl." The groom's father and uncle visit the bride's family with sweets and tea. Acceptance is signalled by sharing tea; refusal by serving water.
  2. Nikâh — the religious marriage rite, performed quietly in the bride's home with the imam, the two fathers, and witnesses.
  3. Çetir bağlav — "binding the tent." The night before the public wedding, the bride's friends gather at her home to braid her hair, paint her hands with henna, and sing the çetir bağlav yırı — half farewell, half blessing.
  4. Toy — the wedding feast itself. Begins with the formal arrival of the groom's procession, includes pahlava, kobete, çibörek, sarma, plov, and inevitably ends in a long Haytarma — danced by the entire community.
  5. Qız ketiruv — the bride's departure to the groom's home, with traditional songs of leavetaking and the lighting of a fire she steps over.

Sürgün Day — 18 May

On 18 May, Crimean Tatars worldwide observe the anniversary of the 1944 deportation. Black ribbons are worn. Candles are lit at sundown. In Crimean cemeteries and in the cemeteries of Uzbekistan, families gather at the graves of those who did not survive the transport or the first years in exile. In Aqmescit (Simferopol) and in cities of the diaspora, public readings of the names of the dead can last more than twelve hours.

It is a day not of nationalism but of grief, and the Crimean Tatar people have built no monument to themselves from it.
