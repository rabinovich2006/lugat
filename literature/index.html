Literature & Poetry

Edebiyat

A literature shaped by reform, revolution, repression, and remembrance.

From İsmail Gaspıralı's reformist journalism in Tercüman to Cengiz Dağcı's novels of exile and Şamil Alâdin's stewardship of the language through the deportation years, Crimean Tatar literature has been an act of survival. The themes return again and again: land, language, and the long question of what is owed to the dead.

The Crimean Tatar literary tradition has three roots — a millennium of Turkic oral poetry (the destans of Çora Batır and Köroğlu, the song-form çıñ); the courtly literature of the Crimean Khanate (16th–18th centuries, in Ottoman-Turkic); and the modern literary norm developed since Gaspıralı's reforms of the 1880s.

The National Anthem

Ant etkenmen
Noman Çelebicihan · 1917 · Sung at every Crimean Tatar gathering
Ant etkenmen, milletimniñ yarasını sarmağa,
Nasıl olsun bu zavallı qardaşlarım çürüsin?
Onlar içün ökünmesem, qayğırmasam, yaşasam,
Yüregimden qara qanlar aqsın, men de çürüyim.
I have sworn to bind the wounds of my people.
How can these poor brothers of mine be left to wither?
If I do not grieve for them, do not care, and yet live on —
let black blood pour from my heart and let me wither too.
Ant etkenmen, şu qaranğı yurtqa şavle sepmege,
Nasıl olsun bu eki qardaş bir-birini körmesin?
Bunı körip buvsanmasam, muğaymasam, yanmasam,
Közyaşları içinde, ah, men de batıp boğulayım.
I have sworn to spread light upon this darkened homeland.
How can it be that these two brothers do not see one another?
If I see it and do not choke, do not mourn, do not burn with grief,
let me drown — let me sink in my own tears.
Ant etkenmen, sözüm-sözdür, milletimni qutarmağa,
Onı çekmek yaşasıñ da, ölmek olsa, ölmege.
Toğru sözden men qaytsam, milletimni aldatsam,
Yer yarılsın, ekiyüzlü, mezarsız da kömülmege.
I have sworn — my word is my word — to save my people.
Whether to live for it, or to die for it, so be it.
If I turn back from the true word, if I betray my people —
let the earth split open, two-faced, and bury me without a grave.

Çelebicihan was executed by Bolshevik forces in Sevastopol on 23 February 1918, a few weeks after writing the third stanza.

Featured poems & passages

Boran
Bekir Çoban-zade · 1929 · From the eponymous collection
Boran qoptı, dağlar saña inildey,
Tüy üstünde at çapışı eşitilmey.
Yalğız bir oraq sesi, sırıltı,
Halqımıznıñ yarasını tikleydi.
A storm has risen; the mountains moan for you.
The hoofbeats of horses upon snow are no longer heard.
Only the sound of a lone scythe, a whisper —
sewing up the wound of my people.
Ey güzel Qırım
Folk song · Composed in exile, c. 1950s · Sung at every Crimean Tatar gathering
Aluştadan esken yelinim,
Yüzlerimni süe, kelinim.
Üyqudan uyanıp ben, bir köreyim —
Vatanımdır, qaytalıqman, ey güzel Qırım.
The wind that blows from Aluşta —
it strokes my face, my daughter-in-law.
Let me wake from sleep and see, just once —
you are my homeland, I will return to you, oh beautiful Crimea.

Featured writers

1851–1914

İsmail Gaspıralı

Journalist, essayist, novelist. Founder of "Tercüman" and author of the bilingual journal "Alem-i Nisvan" (Women's World). His novel "Frenkistan Mektupları" (Letters from Europe) is one of the first modern works of Crimean Tatar prose.

1885–1918

Noman Çelebicihan

Lawyer, statesman, and poet. Author of the Crimean Tatar national anthem "Ant etkenmen" ("I have sworn"). His verse is short, fierce, and prophetic.

1893–1937

Bekir Çoban-zade

Turkologist, poet, founder of modern literary Crimean Tatar. His verse blends Ottoman lyrical tradition with Soviet-era modernism. Executed in the Stalinist purges; rehabilitated 1957.

1912–1996

Şamil Alâdin

Novelist, editor of the literary journal "Yıldız" (1980–1985), keeper of the language through exile. His two-volume Selected Works appeared in 1976–1977.

1920–2011

Cengiz Dağcı

The most widely read Crimean Tatar novelist of the twentieth century. "Korkunç Yıllar," "Yurdunu Kaybeden Adam," "Onlar da İnsandı" — works that brought the deportation to world literature.

1908–1978

Eşref Şemi-zade

Major Soviet-era Crimean Tatar poet, novelist, and translator. His epic verse and historical novels remain central to school curricula in the language today.

A nation lives in its language. — İsmail Gaspıralı, "Tercüman", 1883
