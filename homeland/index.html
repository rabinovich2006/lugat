Vatan — Qırım

Crimea in maps, mountains, and the village names that survived 1944.

Ay-Petri

The mountain over the southern coast of Crimea — sacred in Crimean Tatar tradition, said to take its name from the Christian Saint Peter (Ay Petri).

Geography

Crimea is a peninsula on the northern coast of the Black Sea, about 27,000 km² in area. Its three regions — the northern steppe, the central mountains, and the southern coast (Yalı boyu) — produce three different ways of life and the three Crimean Tatar dialects that match them.

Major sites of Crimean Tatar history

  • Bahçesaray (Бахчисарай) — the Khanate capital. Han Cami, Hansaray (Khan's Palace), Zincirli Medrese, the Fountain of Tears.
  • Aqmescit (Симферополь / Simferopol) — the present-day administrative centre; the seat of the Mejlis until 2014.
  • Kefe (Феодосия / Feodosia) — ancient Caffa, the great Genoese-then-Ottoman port; Çoban-zade was born nearby.
  • Kezleve (Евпатория / Yevpatoriya) — site of the Cuma-Cami built by Mimar Sinan in 1564.
  • Sevastopol — site of Çelebicihan's execution in 1918.
  • Mangup — the cave city in the Crimean Mountains, a former Crimean Tatar stronghold.
  • Ay Petri — the mountain over the southern coast, sacred in Crimean Tatar tradition.

The old village names

After the 1944 deportation, the Soviet authorities renamed virtually every Crimean Tatar village. Crimean Tatars continue to use the older names — Aluşta, Sudak, Yalta have kept their identifiable forms; Karasubazar became Belogorsk, Stary Krym became Eski Qırım again in common Crimean Tatar speech.
