Cuisine

Aşhane

Çibörek, sarma, kobete, pahlava — twelve essential dishes of the Crimean Tatar table.

Crimean Tatar cuisine drew on Black Sea, Ottoman, Mongol, and Central Asian traditions long before the Sürgün scattered it. The cuisine has three layers, distilled over six centuries: the meat-and-dairy economy of the Crimean steppe (mutton, lamb, fermented mare's milk, dried curd); the fruits, nuts, herbs, and seafood of the southern coast and mountains; and the rich pastry tradition shared with the Ottoman world. After 1944 the recipes were carried into exile and adapted with the ingredients of Uzbekistan, Kazakhstan, and Turkey. They have come back changed — and unchanged.

The Crimean Tatar table is hospitable and slow. A guest who steps into a Crimean Tatar home is not allowed to leave hungry. The phrase "Ev qonağı — Allah qonağı" — "A house guest is God's guest" — is the moral grammar of every meal.

Featured recipes

Çibörek

Crimean Tatar fried meat pastry · Serves 6 (makes about 12 pastries)
45 minprep
15 mincook
Easytechnique

The most iconic Crimean Tatar dish. A half-moon turnover of paper-thin dough wrapped around seasoned minced lamb (or beef) and onion, then deep-fried until the surface blisters into golden domes. The bubble — qabuq, the "shell" — is the sign that the çibörek is done correctly.

Ingredients · Dough

  • 500 g (3⅓ cups) all-purpose flour
  • 1 large egg
  • 250 ml (1 cup) warm water
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1 tablespoon vodka or sunflower oil (helps the bubble form)

Ingredients · Filling

  • 500 g (1 lb) lamb shoulder, twice ground — or 50/50 lamb & beef
  • 2 medium onions, very finely grated (must release juice)
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1 teaspoon freshly ground black pepper
  • ½ teaspoon ground cumin (optional)
  • 3 tablespoons cold water — to keep the filling juicy
  • Sunflower oil for deep-frying, about 1 litre

Method

  1. Mix flour and salt. Whisk together egg, water, and vodka or oil. Pour wet into dry. Knead by hand for 8–10 minutes — the dough must be smooth, elastic, and slightly tacky but not sticking. Cover with a damp towel and rest at room temperature for 30 minutes.
  2. While the dough rests, grate the onions over the meat. Add salt, pepper, cumin, and the cold water. Mix with your hands until the filling is wet and slightly loose — this is critical for the famous juice that bursts out at the first bite.
  3. Divide the dough into 12 equal balls. On a lightly floured surface, roll each ball into a thin round about 18 cm (7 in) in diameter. Aim for paper-thin — you should almost see through it.
  4. Place a generous tablespoon of filling on one half of each round, spreading it into a half-moon shape and leaving a 1.5 cm border. Fold the dough over and press the edge firmly. Crimp with the tines of a fork — and most importantly, with your fingertips — to seal completely. Any breach will let the filling leak.
  5. Heat oil in a wide pan to 180 °C (350 °F) — about a 4 cm depth. Slide in two çibörek at a time. They should puff and balloon immediately. Fry 1–2 minutes per side, until deep golden with darker blisters.
  6. Drain briefly on paper. Serve hot in a stack, dusted with a little black pepper. The classic accompaniment is a small glass of ayran (salted yoghurt drink) and pickled cabbage.

Cultural note: Eating çibörek is a deliberate act. Bite the corner first to let the steam and juice out; tilt the pastry to drink the broth from the meat-and-onion before biting in fully. The most respectful compliment a guest can pay the cook is to ask, smiling and slightly burned, for a second.

Sarma

Stuffed vine or cabbage leaves · Serves 6
1 hrprep
1½ hrscook
Mediumtechnique

Sarma is the patient cousin of çibörek — a dish that rewards careful rolling and slow simmering. Vine leaves are preferred in summer; cabbage in winter. The filling is plain on purpose: rice, minced meat, onion, herbs, a little tomato.

Ingredients

  • About 60 fresh or brined grape vine leaves, OR 1 large head of cabbage
  • 250 g (1¼ cups) short-grain rice, rinsed
  • 500 g (1 lb) ground lamb or beef
  • 2 onions, finely chopped
  • 50 g (¼ cup) butter or 3 tbsp olive oil
  • 2 tbsp tomato paste
  • Small bunch dill, chopped
  • Small bunch parsley, chopped
  • 1 tsp salt, ½ tsp black pepper, ½ tsp dried mint
  • 1 litre good lamb or chicken broth, hot
  • To serve: thick yoghurt, a little crushed garlic, dried mint

Method

  1. If using brined leaves, soak in warm water for 10 minutes, then rinse. If using cabbage, plunge the whole head into boiling water and peel leaves off as they soften.
  2. Cook the onions in butter or oil until translucent. Add tomato paste and stir for 1 minute. Cool slightly.
  3. Mix the rice, meat, cooled onion mixture, herbs, salt, pepper, and mint. Knead briefly.
  4. Place a leaf shiny-side down. At the stem end, put 1 tablespoon filling. Fold the sides in and roll up tightly — like a small green cigar. Repeat with all leaves.
  5. Line the bottom of a wide pot with the toughest or torn leaves. Pack rolled sarma seam-side down in tight concentric circles, layered. Pour over the hot broth — it should just cover.
  6. Weight with an inverted plate so the rolls do not unfurl. Bring to a gentle simmer, cover, and cook 1–1½ hours on the lowest heat. The rice should be tender, the leaves silky.
  7. Serve warm, not boiling, with yoghurt-garlic-mint sauce alongside.

Pahlava

Honey-and-walnut layered pastry · Makes one 30 × 23 cm tray, about 20 diamonds
1 hrprep
45 minbake
Mediumtechnique

The Crimean Tatar version of baklava — sweeter than the Turkish, often perfumed with rose water. Made for weddings, Hıdırlez, and the two Bayrams.

Ingredients

  • 1 package (450 g) phyllo dough, thawed
  • 300 g (10 oz) walnuts, finely chopped
  • 2 tbsp sugar (for the filling)
  • 1 tsp ground cinnamon
  • 250 g (1 cup + 2 tbsp) unsalted butter, melted

Syrup

  • 250 ml (1 cup) honey
  • 250 ml (1 cup) water
  • 200 g (1 cup) sugar
  • 1 tablespoon lemon juice
  • 1 teaspoon rose water (optional)

Method

  1. Preheat oven to 175 °C (350 °F). Brush a 30 × 23 cm tray with melted butter.
  2. Combine the chopped walnuts with 2 tbsp sugar and cinnamon.
  3. Lay one sheet of phyllo in the tray. Brush with butter. Repeat for 8 sheets, buttering each.
  4. Spread a third of the walnut filling over the buttered sheets.
  5. Add 6 more buttered sheets, then another third of the filling. Add 6 more buttered sheets, then the rest of the filling. Finish with 8 final buttered sheets on top.
  6. With a sharp knife, cut into diamonds — all the way through to the bottom. Brush the top with the remaining butter.
  7. Bake 40–45 minutes, until deep golden.
  8. While the pahlava bakes, combine syrup ingredients in a saucepan. Simmer 10 minutes, then take off the heat and add rose water if using.
  9. The moment the pahlava comes out of the oven, pour the cool (not boiling) syrup evenly over the hot pastry. Listen for the hiss — that is what makes the layers shatter and absorb. Let stand at least 3 hours, ideally overnight.

Cultural note: Cut the pahlava only just before serving — once cut, it should be eaten the same day. Offering pahlava and a small cup of Turkish coffee or strong black tea is the universal gesture of welcome in a Crimean Tatar home.

The full repertoire

Çibörek

Crimean Tatar meat pastry

The most iconic Crimean Tatar dish: a half-moon turnover of thin dough filled with seasoned minced lamb or beef and onion, deep-fried until the surface blisters into golden domes. Çibörek travelled with the deportation and is now a staple from Bahçesaray to Tashkent.

Yantıq

Pan-fried version of çibörek

Yantıq is çibörek's elegant cousin: the same dough and filling, but dry-cooked on a hot pan rather than deep-fried. Brushed with butter or yoghurt after cooking. Considered the everyday family version of the dish.

Sarma

Stuffed vine or cabbage leaves

Tender vine or cabbage leaves rolled around rice, minced meat, onion, and herbs, then simmered in broth. Traditionally served with kaymak (clotted cream) and yoghurt-mint sauce.

Kobete

Layered meat pie

A large round pie of multiple thin pastry layers filled with lamb or chicken, onion, and broth — the most ceremonial dish of Crimean Tatar cuisine, often served at weddings and major holidays.

Şuberek

Boiled and pan-fried meat dumpling

Thin half-moon dumplings filled with seasoned meat, briefly boiled and then crisped on a hot pan. Lighter than çibörek but rich in flavour.

Lağman

Hand-pulled noodles with lamb

A meaty soup-stew of thick hand-pulled noodles, lamb, vegetables, and pepper. Adopted from Central Asian neighbours during exile and now part of the Crimean Tatar repertoire.

Plov

Rice with lamb and carrots

Crimean Tatar plov favours long-grain rice, lamb, carrots cut into matchsticks, and the slow cooking style learned and refined during the Uzbekistan years.

Kavurma

Slow-cooked lamb in its fat

Lamb is browned, then simmered for hours in its own rendered fat with onion, salt, and black pepper — a winter dish that travels and keeps.

Samsa

Baked meat or pumpkin pastry

Triangular or rectangular pastries baked in a tandyr oven, filled with meat and onion, or with sweet pumpkin. Crackling crust, tender filling.

Peremech

Open-faced meat pastry

A round flatbread with a meat filling pressed into the centre and an open top. Pan-fried in oil; served with yoghurt or tea.

Pahlava

Honey-and-walnut layered pastry

The Crimean Tatar version of baklava: layers of thin pastry, ground walnut, and honey syrup. Sweeter than Turkish baklava, often perfumed with rose water.

Şeker Qıyıq

Fried sweet bow-knots

Twisted strips of egg-rich dough fried in oil and dusted with powdered sugar — a wedding sweet, also made for Hıdırlez and Bayrams.
