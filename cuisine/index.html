Cuisine

Aşhane

Çibörek, sarma, kobete, pahlava — twelve essential dishes of the Crimean Tatar table.

Crimean Tatar cuisine drew on Black Sea, Ottoman, and Central Asian traditions long before the Sürgün scattered it. After 1944 the recipes were carried into exile and adapted with the ingredients of Uzbekistan and Turkey. They have come back changed — and unchanged.

Çibörek

Crimean Tatar meat pastry

The most iconic Crimean Tatar dish: a half-moon turnover of thin dough filled with seasoned minced lamb or beef and onion, deep-fried until the surface blisters into golden domes. Çibörek travelled with the deportation and is now a staple from Bahçesaray to Tashkent.

Yantıq

Pan-fried version of çibörek

Yantıq is çibörek's elegant cousin: the same dough and filling, but dry-cooked on a hot pan rather than deep-fried. Brushed with butter or yoghurt after cooking. Considered the everyday family version of the dish.

Sarma

Stuffed vine or cabbage leaves

Tender vine or cabbage leaves rolled around rice, minced meat, onion, and herbs, then simmered in broth. Traditionally served with kaymak (clotted cream) and yoghurt-mint sauce.

Kobete

Layered meat pie

A large round pie of multiple thin pastry layers filled with lamb or chicken, onion, and broth — the most ceremonial dish of Crimean Tatar cuisine, often served at weddings and major holidays.

Şuberek

Boiled and pan-fried meat dumpling

Thin half-moon dumplings filled with seasoned meat, briefly boiled and then crisped on a hot pan. Lighter than çibörek but rich in flavour.

Lağman

Hand-pulled noodles with lamb

A meaty soup-stew of thick hand-pulled noodles, lamb, vegetables, and pepper. Adopted from Central Asian neighbours during exile and now part of the Crimean Tatar repertoire.

Plov

Rice with lamb and carrots

Crimean Tatar plov favours long-grain rice, lamb, carrots cut into matchsticks, and the slow cooking style learned and refined during the Uzbekistan years.

Kavurma

Slow-cooked lamb in its fat

Lamb is browned, then simmered for hours in its own rendered fat with onion, salt, and black pepper — a winter dish that travels and keeps.

Samsa

Baked meat or pumpkin pastry

Triangular or rectangular pastries baked in a tandyr oven, filled with meat and onion, or with sweet pumpkin. Crackling crust, tender filling.

Peremech

Open-faced meat pastry

A round flatbread with a meat filling pressed into the centre and an open top. Pan-fried in oil; served with yoghurt or tea.

Pahlava

Honey-and-walnut layered pastry

The Crimean Tatar version of baklava: layers of thin pastry, ground walnut, and honey syrup. Sweeter than Turkish baklava, often perfumed with rose water.

Şeker Qıyıq

Fried sweet bow-knots

Twisted strips of egg-rich dough fried in oil and dusted with powdered sugar — a wedding sweet, also made for Hıdırlez and Bayrams.
