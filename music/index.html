Music & Dance

Çalğı ve Oyun

The saz, the dare, the Haytarma — instruments and dances that travelled into exile and came back.

Crimean Tatar music has carried memory across half a century of exile. The Haytarma — both a dance and a word meaning "to return" — became a quiet act of resistance in Tashkent and Samarkand: as long as the music was sung, the homeland still lived.

The musical tradition has three roots: the long Turkic-Tatar folk repertoire of çıñ (lyrical song) and destan (heroic epic); the courtly maqam tradition of the Khanate, performed at the Hansaray; and the rich diaspora repertoire that grew in Uzbekistan and Turkey through the second half of the 20th century. Many of the most loved Crimean Tatar songs today — "Ey güzel Qırım," "Şu bağçada" — were written or arranged in exile and only returned home with the people.

Haytarma — the dance of return

The Haytarma is the most iconic Crimean Tatar dance. It is danced in pairs — a man and a woman — with the man circling in measured steps while the woman, in a richly embroidered dress and a long-sleeved jacket, spins on her toes with arms lifted overhead like a flame. The choreography belongs to the second half of the 19th century, formalised in the early Soviet years by the Crimean State Ensemble.

The word haytarma means "to return." After 1944, when the Crimean Tatar people had been deported and the word "Crimean Tatar" had been struck from Soviet maps, the music continued — first quietly in private homes in Uzbekistan, then in the State Tatar Ensemble of Tashkent, then in every gathering of every diaspora community. To dance the Haytarma was — and is — an act of remembering that the return is not over.

The 2013 feature film Haytarma, directed by Akhtem Seitablaev, is named for the dance. Its central scene shows the moment when, in the middle of the cattle-car journey east in May 1944, a young woman begins to dance.

Songs & dance forms

Haytarma

The most iconic Crimean Tatar dance. Wedding-pair circles, intricate footwork, dazzling spins by the female partner. After 1944 the word became charged: it means "to return."

Çıñ

Crimean Tatar folk song form — a short lyrical verse traditionally sung at gatherings, in private, or during seasonal festivals. Hundreds of çıñ survive from the pre-war repertoire alone.

Qaytarma

A rhythmic dance form, often the climax of an evening of music. "Qaytarma" too means "to return" — a quiet but persistent rhyme through the music of the exile years.

Ey güzel Qırım

"Oh beautiful Crimea" — perhaps the most beloved Crimean Tatar song. Composed in the post-war years; learned by every Crimean Tatar child and sung at every gathering.

Şu Bağçada

A folk song about a particular garden, a particular yearning. The melody is melancholic, the lyrics are wry — a perfect example of çıñ form.

Contemporary musicians

Jamala (Susana Jamaladinova, b. 1983) — Crimean Tatar singer and jazz composer; won Eurovision 2016 with "1944," about her great-grandmother's deportation. Her grandmother survived the journey but lost a young daughter on the way. The song's chorus — "yaşlığıma toyalmadım, men bu yerde yaşalmadım" ("I could not get my fill of youth, I could not live in this place") — is the dead child's voice.

Rüstem Memetov — leading saz player and composer of the post-deportation generation; founder of the Crimean Tatar State Ensemble in independent Ukraine.

Eldar Memetov, Süleyman Memetov, Edie Üseinov — instrumentalists keeping the maqam and çıñ traditions alive today.

ATR Music — the music programming arm of the Crimean Tatar television channel; recorded and broadcast Crimean Tatar music to a global audience until forced into exile in 2015. Its YouTube archive has become the largest public collection of Crimean Tatar musical recordings.

Instruments

Saz

Long-necked lute, the heart of Crimean Tatar music. Eight or twelve strings, plucked with a tortoise-shell pick. Used both for solo accompaniment and for ensemble playing.

Dare

Frame drum with metal jingles, played with both hands. The rhythmic backbone of Haytarma.

Qaval

End-blown wooden flute — the instrument most associated with pastoral and contemplative pieces.

Davul

Large double-headed drum, played with sticks. Used for outdoor processions and festive music.

Skripka

Violin — Crimean Tatar tradition adopted the violin extensively in the 19th and 20th centuries; central to dance music.

Akordeon

Accordion — a Soviet-era addition that took deep root in exile, accompanying singing and dance at every gathering in Uzbekistan.
