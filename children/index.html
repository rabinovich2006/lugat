Children's Corner

Balalar köşesi

Çora Batır, Altın Beşik, ayneni — the stories and songs Crimean Tatar grandparents pass on.

Folk tales, lullabies, nursery rhymes, and the picture dictionary — the building blocks of a child's relationship with Crimean Tatar language and culture. Many of these stories were nearly lost; in exile they survived as bedtime stories told from memory, written down only later. They are still being collected today.

Folk tales — brief introductions

Çora Batır

The hero of Crimean Tatar oral tradition. A foundling raised by an old couple grows into the warrior who liberates the Crimean Tatar people from their enemies. The destan (heroic poem) cycles around Çora's battles, his magical horse, and his eventual fate.

Tepegöz

The one-eyed giant — a story shared across Turkic peoples. Tepegöz holds shepherds captive in his cave; only the cleverest can blind him and escape past his flocks. A Crimean Tatar parallel to the Cyclops episode in Homer.

Altın Beşik

"The golden cradle." A magical infant in a golden cradle is hidden in the mountains of Crimea, watched over by spirits. Whoever finds the cradle finds the future of the people — but no one is yet worthy. A story of patience, exile, and return.

Üç Qardaş

"Three brothers." The youngest and gentlest brother proves wiser and more courageous than the older two. A classic Crimean Tatar tale of fairness, cleverness, and respect for one's elders.

Çora Batır — a retelling

Çora Batır

The hero who returned · a Crimean Tatar destan, retold for children

Long ago, in a Crimean Tatar village by a stream, there lived an old shepherd named Narik and his wife Menli. They had no children, though they had prayed for one for forty years. One spring morning, as Menli was drawing water at the stream, she heard a baby's cry coming from a basket of woven reeds floating downstream. She caught the basket, pulled it to shore, and inside found a boy with hair the colour of wheat and eyes the colour of the Black Sea. They called him Çora, and they raised him as their own.

Çora grew quickly — and strangely. By the time he was seven he was as tall and as strong as the men of the village. By the time he was twelve he could lift a horse onto his shoulders. The villagers, who had at first been pleased at the shepherd's good fortune, began to whisper that the boy was not their kind.

It was in those days that the Khanate of Qırım fell upon hard times. Raiders from far away — from across the steppe — were stealing horses, burning villages, and carrying away the young women. The Khan's men could not stop them. The Crimean Tatar people were afraid.

"I will go," said Çora to his foster parents. "I will go and stop them."

"You are still a boy," said Narik. "You have no horse and no sword."

"I will find them," said Çora.

He walked for forty days into the foothills and there he found, deep in a cave, an old warrior's armour and beside it a horse that had been waiting for a master for a hundred years. The horse knew him; it lowered its head, and Çora mounted. The armour fit him as if it had been forged for his shoulders.

Çora rode against the raiders three times, and three times he turned them back. Each time he returned, the Khan offered him gold; each time he refused, asking only for the freedom of the women they had taken. The captives walked home with him along the road to Bahçesaray.

The raiders, defeated, decided to send not warriors but trickery. They sent a beautiful woman to the Khan's court with a false message; she pretended to fall in love with Çora; she lulled him to sleep with words and wine; and while he slept, she stole his sword and led him into a trap from which only the gallop of his faithful horse could save him.

Çora woke, fought his way out, and rode back to the foothills. There, in the cave, he laid down the sword and the armour exactly where he had found them, and rode his horse out across the steppe, eastward — toward the place where the sun rises. Some say he died of his wounds there. Some say he lives still, asleep in a golden cradle in the Crimean mountains, waiting to be needed again.

"Çora Batır qaytacaq" — Çora the hero will return. It is what Crimean Tatar children have whispered to each other for centuries, looking up at the mountains.

Lullabies — Ayneni

The Crimean Tatar lullaby form is called ayneni — short, sweet, repetitive verses that mothers and grandmothers have sung for centuries. The classic refrain is "Ayneni, ayneni, balam ayneni" ("Hush, hush, my little one"). Most of the surviving lullabies were preserved by grandmothers in exile, who sang to their grandchildren the songs their own grandmothers had sung in Crimea.

A grandmother's ayneni
Anonymous · collected by Crimean Tatar folklorists, 1970s
Ayneni, ayneni,
Balam ayneni —
Anañ keldi, qaytarmadı
Ayrılmaz balasını.
Hush, hush, hush —
my child, hush.
Your mother came, and she did not take with her
the child she will never leave.
Beşigiñ altın, telli olsun,
Atañ aman, anañ saçlı.
Yastığıñ dağ, yorğanıñ deryâ,
Yat balam, yat balam, yat.
May your cradle be gold and silken,
your father safe, your mother long-haired.
Your pillow the mountain, your blanket the sea —
sleep, my little one, sleep.

Nursery rhymes & counting

Crimean Tatar children learn to count with a small song that begins:

Bir, eki, üç, dört —
Mışıq başın oynat!
Beş, altı, yedi, sekiz —
Ayı bal alır kekiz!

One, two, three, four — kitten, shake your head! Five, six, seven, eight — the bear takes honey from the hive!

Traditional games

  • Aşıq — knucklebones. A game played with the small bones of a sheep's ankle, on a flat patch of ground. Skill, throw, gamble. The Crimean Tatar version has a known regional name for each of the four possible positions of the bone.
  • Cıyın — a circle clapping-and-singing game played at family gatherings. Children take turns naming an animal or a colour as the rhythm carries on; whoever falters joins the centre and begins again.
  • Top oyunu — the ball game. A small leather ball is passed by foot between players standing in a circle, with the loser doing a small Haytarma step at the end. Common at Hıdırlez.
  • Yağlı sırıq — the greased pole. A wooden pole greased with sheep fat is stood up in the meadow at Hıdırlez. A prize — usually a chicken or a small purse of coins — is tied to the top. Boys take turns trying to climb. It rarely takes less than an hour.
